<p>By <a href="http://twitter.com/vicapow">Victor Powell</a> and <a href="http://twitter.com/lewislehe">Lewis Lehe</a>
</p>
<p>
</p><p>Eigenvalues/vectors are instrumental to understanding electrical circuits, mechanical systems, ecology and even Google&#39;s <a href="http://www.rose-hulman.edu/~bryan/googleFinalVersionFixed.pdf">PageRank</a> algorithm. Let&#39;s see if visualization can make these ideas more intuitive.</p>
<p>To begin, let $v$ be a vector (shown as a point) and $A$ be a matrix with columns $a_1$ and $a_2$ (shown as arrows). If we multiply $v$ by $A$, then $A$ sends $v$ to a new vector $Av$.</p>

<p><p>If you can draw a line through the three points $(0,0)$, $v$ and $Av$, then $Av$ is just $v$ multiplied by a number $\lambda$; that is, $Av = \lambda v$. In this case, we call $\lambda$ an <strong>eigenvalue</strong> and $v$ an <strong>eigenvector</strong>. For example, here $(1,2)$ is an eigvector and $5$ an eigenvalue.</p>

</p>
<script type="math/tex; mode=display">
A v =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
8 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\cdot
\begin{pmatrix}
1 \\ 2
\end{pmatrix}
=
5
\begin{pmatrix}
1 \\ 2
\end{pmatrix}
= \lambda v.
</script><p>Below, change the columns of $A$ and drag $v$ to be an eigenvector. Note three facts: First, every point on the same line as an eigenvector is an eigenvector. Those lines are <strong>eigenspaces</strong>, and each has an associated eigenvalue. Second, if you place $v$ on an eigenspace (either $s_1$ or $s_2$) with associated eigenvalue $\lambda<1$, then $Av$ is closer to $(0,0)$ than $v$; but when $\lambda>1$, it&#39;s farther. Third, both eigenspaces depend on both columns of $A$: it is not as though $a_1$ only affects $s_1$.</p>

<div ng-controller="TransCtrl">
<simple-plot></simple-plot>
<p><h3 id="what-are-eigenvalues-vectors-good-for-">What are eigenvalues/vectors good for?</h3>
<p>If you keep multiplying $v$ by $A$, you get a sequence ${ v, Av, A^2v,}$ etc. Eigenspaces attract that sequence and eigenvalues tell you whether it ends up at $(0,0)$ or far away. Therefore, eigenvectors/values tell us about systems that evolve step-by-step.</p>

</p>
<div ng-controller="PopulationCtrl">
<simple-plot></simple-plot>
</div>
<p><p>Let&#39;s explore some applications and properties of these sequences.</p>

</p>

<p><h3 id="fibonacci-sequence">Fibonacci Sequence</h3>
<p>Suppose you have some amoebas in a petri dish. Every minute, all adult amoebas produce one child amoeba, and all child amoebas grow into adults (Note: this is not really how amoebas reproduce.). So if $t$ is a minute, the equation of this system is</p>

<script type="math/tex; mode=display">
\begin{eqnarray}
\text{adults}_{t+1} & = & \text{adults}_{t} + \text{children}_{t} \\
\text{children}_{t+1} & = & \text{adults}_{t}
\end{eqnarray}
</script><p>which we can rewrite in matrix form like</p>

<script type="math/tex; mode=display">
\begin{eqnarray}
v_{t+1} &=& A & ⋅& v_t
\\
\begin{pmatrix}
\text{adults}_{t+1} \\ \text{children}_{t+1}
\end{pmatrix}
&=&
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}
& \cdot &
\begin{pmatrix}
\text{adults}_{t} \\
\text{children}_{t}
\end{pmatrix}
\end{eqnarray}
</script><p>Below, press &quot;Forward&quot; to step ahead a minute. The total population is the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci_number">Fibonacci Sequence</a>.</p>

</p>

<h3 id="steady-states">Steady States</h3>
<p>Suppose that, every year, a fraction $p$ of New Yorkers move to California and a fraction $q$ of Californians move to New York. Drag the circles to decide these fractions and the number starting in each state.</p>

</p>
</section>
<section ng-controller="MigrationCtrl">
<sf-to-ny-migration-map></sf-to-ny-migration-map>
<p><p>To understand the system better, we can start by writing it in matrix terms like:</p>

</p>
<script type="math/tex; mode=display">
\begin{eqnarray}
v_{t+1} & = & Av_t
\\
\begin{pmatrix}
\text{New York}_{t+1} \\ \text{California}_{t+1}
\end{pmatrix}
&=&
\begin{pmatrix}
1-p & q \\
p & 1-q
\end{pmatrix}
\cdot
\begin{pmatrix}
\text{New York}_{t} \\
\text{California}_{t}
\end{pmatrix}
\end{eqnarray}
</script>
<p><p>It turns out that a matrix like $A$, whose entries are positive and whose columns add up to one (try it!), is called a <a href="http://www.math.harvard.edu/~knill/teaching/math19b_2011/handouts/lecture33.pdf">Markov matrix</a>, and it always has $\lambda = 1$ as its largest eigenvalue. That means there&#39;s a value of $v_t$ for which $Av_t =\lambda v_t = 1 v_t = v_t$. At this &quot;steady state,&quot; the same number of people move in each direction, and the populations stay the same forever. Hover over the animation to see the system go to the steady state.</p>

</p>

<h1 id="complex-eigenvalues">Complex eigenvalues</h1>
<p>So far we&#39;ve only looked at systems with real eigenvalues. But looking at the equation $ Av = \lambda v$, who&#39;s to say $\lambda$ and $v$ can&#39;t have some imaginary part? That it can&#39;t be a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number">complex</a> number? For example, </p>

<script type="math/tex; mode=display">
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
-1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\cdot
\begin{pmatrix}
1 \\ i
\end{pmatrix}
=
(1+i)
\cdot
\begin{pmatrix}
1 \\ i
\end{pmatrix}.
</script><p>Here, $1+i$ is an eigenvalue and $(1, i)$ is an eigenvector. </p>
<p>If a matrix has complex eigenvalues, its sequence spirals around $(0,0)$. To see this, drag $A$&#39;s columns (the arrows) around until you get a spiral. The eigenvalues are plotted in the real/imaginary plane to the right. You&#39;ll see that whenever the eigenvalues have an imaginary part, the system spirals, no matter where you start things off.</p>
